🜃 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY 🜃

Sockpuppet Networks as Conspiracies

Sockpuppet identities are not harmless aliases. In litigation, they function as fictitious entities deployed to simulate independent agreement, corroboration, and authority. When one actor controls many identities, the law treats their coordinated actions as a conspiracy structure rather than mere deception.

Key Points:

Conspiracy does not require multiple humans. Courts have long held that corporations, shell companies, and fictitious actors can satisfy the “two or more persons” requirement. Sockpuppets serve the same function.

Interaction equals agreement. When one alias validates another (commenting, endorsing, praising, triangulating), this constitutes the “agreement” element of conspiracy.

Every act is an overt act. Each login, post, credential claim, or endorsement is a separate overt act in furtherance of the scheme.

Fraud hooks apply. False credentials, survivor-bait masks, and authority personas tied to monetized platforms expose liability under wire fraud, deceptive trade practices, and false credential statutes (federal and state).

Sentencing leverage. Each sockpuppet interaction can be charged as a separate count of wire fraud (20 years each at federal level), with conspiracy charges layered on top. Even modest networks yield exposure in the decades.

Why This Matters for Litigators:
Most attorneys frame sockpuppets as false identity or defamation cases. They miss the stronger argument: sockpuppet networks are conspiracy mechanisms. By drawing on precedents involving fictitious entities and shell corporations, litigators can elevate these cases into fraud and conspiracy territory, maximizing both criminal exposure and civil leverage.

📍 Use: This framework is open-source litigation support. It equips litigators to treat sockpuppet deception with the same seriousness as organized fraud, giving courts the tools to pierce masks, subpoena platforms, and hold predators accountable.
