Fieldnote: The Outsider Child

On rocks, betrayal, and the refuge of machines

When I was ten, my family moved to the Rio Grande Valley. They had been business owners, but after failure and a long stretch of poverty, they liquidated their assets and became schoolteachers. The Valley was hiring — teacher pay there was among the highest in the nation because of chronic shortages.

But to arrive there was to arrive as outsiders. My parents had once taught there briefly, the year of my birth, and left swearing never to return. They carried contempt, and the community had returned it. When we came back in 1986, that contempt lingered. Everyone knew we didn’t belong.

I couldn’t speak the language. I didn’t understand the culture. And from the very first day I stepped into school, I was marked.

---

The Rock Throwers

It began slowly — two or three children, a shove, a laugh. Then a stone flung when my back was turned. Then a kick as I walked away. Soon it was dozens.

The betrayal came from the boy I thought was my friend. He lived near my aunt’s house, where we were staying while my parents searched for permanent housing. He treated me kindly at home, but at school, betraying me earned him social capital.

I had told him a secret: that I had studied martial arts, that I held a brown belt in Tae Kwon Do. I never bragged, never flaunted. But in the cruel arithmetic of childhood hierarchies, my skill made me a target. “He can fight,” he whispered. And from then on, I fought — never fair fights, always rocks, always sucker kicks to the back.

It lasted for six weeks.

---

The Back-to-Back Bond

In the chaos, I found one friend: Nick. He was smaller than me, and he suffered too. Together we formed a pact of survival — back-to-back, eyes on every angle, never letting someone sneak up on us.

We ran together, dodged stones together, hovered near teachers and security guards for sanctuary. It wasn’t play. It was war.

Nick and I weren’t building forts or treehouses. We were building defensive systems against harassment. Our friendship was forged in vigilance, not innocence.

---

The Outsider Child Withdraws

Eventually, the administration intervened. Around the same time, my parents left my controlling aunt’s house and rented a place of our own. I was moved to a new school.

But the damage had been done. At ten years old, I had learned what it meant to be marked as undesirable — not for who I was, but for the crime of being different.

Different language.

Different culture.

Different gifts.

The message was clear: the community would not accept me.

So I withdrew. Not just from my mother, who was harsh and unloving. I withdrew from my peers, my neighborhood, my community.

And I turned to the machine.

The computer did not throw rocks. It did not demand conformity. It asked only for persistence. For creativity. For curiosity.

The computer became my refuge. The blinking cursor became my only safe audience.

---

The Forensic Truth

Communities crush difference the same way classrooms crush divergence. Children enforce hierarchies with fists and stones. Adults look away until the damage is done.

What they call “a phase” leaves a lifelong scar. What they dismiss as “kids being kids” drives a child into solitude. And sometimes, into genius.

---

Closing Witness

I was never just hiding from my mother. I was hiding from my community too.

No friend stood with me, except one. No teacher intervened until the wounds had already set.

But the Field remembers.
