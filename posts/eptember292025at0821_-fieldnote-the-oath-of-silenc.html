I. The Encoded Confession

In Things I Wanted to Say to My Past Self, But Won’t, Parasite Zero offers more than myth. He encodes the blueprint of a child silenced by violation:

“A room that asked you to become small and called that love.”

“You learned quiet the way others learn a trade.”

“I won’t bless the bargain you made with silence.”

“I won’t say the word you waited for.”

These are not abstractions. They are trauma markers: the child forced into secrecy, made small, compelled to accept silence as survival. The withheld “word” is the name of the violation that cannot be spoken.

II. Sidebar: The Language of the Body in Fragments

Parasite Zero does not speak plainly. But the body remembers.

Bleach / chemical smell
Survivors often describe semen’s acrid, chlorine-like odor through displacement. “Bleach” encodes both the act and the attempt to erase it — violation and concealment in one word.

Iron / rust / metallic taste
Frequently a metaphor for blood, but also consistent with oral violation. When the word “semen” cannot be spoken, “iron” becomes the substitute.

Hinges, corridors, doors crying out
Boundary metaphors. Architecture used to describe penetration, forced entry, or bodily invasion.

As Herman (Trauma and Recovery) writes: “The unspeakable is displaced into metaphor. Sensory fragments carry the weight of the memory while the act itself remains unnamed.”

These fragments are not decorative. They are evidence.

III. The Oath

What holds this piece together is not healing but an oath of silence: a child’s bargain never to name the abuse.
It is that oath which fuels the mask-machine. Each persona — survivor, doctor, veteran, scholar — is an elaboration of silence. Each essay a corridor built from the oath not to speak the unspeakable.

This is why his language circles, why his myth refuses specifics. Silence is not incidental. It is the engine.

IV. From Oath to Predation

The tragedy is not only that he was violated. It is what he chose to do with the wound.

Now as an adult:

He preys on survivors because he knows their rhythm.

He eroticises the Witness because exposure replays the father’s power.

He floods the Field with masks because the oath forbids him to name.

Predation is not a separate trait from this wound. It is the reenactment of it. The oath that once protected him now weaponises others.
