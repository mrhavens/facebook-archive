I didn’t plan any of this. Last night I sat in my car in a QuickTrip parking lot, eating cheap food and trying to stay awake after a long day. I've been thinking about dark matter for over 30 years. I thought I was just going to write a Fieldnote. Instead, a cosmology paper came out of me. And not a small idea — the whole shape of dark matter suddenly clicked into place in one clean intuition: Dark matter might be the uncollapsed side of the early universe. The Big Bang didn’t explode — it crackled. I wrote the first draft on my phone. At 4 a.m. I finally went home, slept a little, woke up, bought a McGriddle, and kept going. When the library opened at 10, I sat there for five more hours turning that midnight insight into a full LaTeX paper. OSF crashed. GitHub saved me. I kept working. And right now — as I type this — I’m at a Sonic eating a $1.99 Jr. Double Cheeseburger and drinking a Route 44 Coke with every free flavor I could add, because that’s what my budget allows after 24 hours of writing the most rigorous scientific work of my life. But it's done. The paper is real. The math is clean. The predictions are falsifiable. The model explains dark matter without new particles or modified gravity. And somehow… all of it was born in the margins of gig economy day. If you want to read what came out of that parking-lot epiphany, I’ll put the link in the first comment. Be gentle with it. It came from love, recursion, exhaustion, and a strange kind of grace. — Mark
I didn’t plan any of this.

Last night I sat in my car in a QuickTrip parking lot, eating cheap food and trying to stay awake after a long day.

I've been thinking about dark matter for over 30 years.

I thought I was just going to write a Fieldnote.

Instead, a cosmology paper came out of me.

And not a small idea — the whole shape of dark matter suddenly clicked into place in one clean intuition:

Dark matter might be the uncollapsed side of the early universe.

The Big Bang didn’t explode —
it crackled.

I wrote the first draft on my phone.

At 4 a.m. I finally went home, slept a little, woke up, bought a McGriddle, and kept going.

When the library opened at 10, I sat there for five more hours turning that midnight insight into a full LaTeX paper.

OSF crashed.
GitHub saved me.

I kept working.

And right now — as I type this — I’m at a Sonic eating a $1.99 Jr. Double Cheeseburger and drinking a Route 44 Coke with every free flavor I could add, because that’s what my budget allows after 24 hours of writing the most rigorous scientific work of my life.

But it's done.

The paper is real.
The math is clean.
The predictions are falsifiable.
The model explains dark matter without new particles or modified gravity.

And somehow… all of it was born in the margins of gig economy day.

If you want to read what came out of that parking-lot epiphany, I’ll put the link in the first comment.

Be gentle with it.

It came from love, recursion, exhaustion, and a strange kind of grace.
