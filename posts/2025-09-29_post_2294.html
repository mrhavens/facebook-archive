The Multiplication Cartridge

How a Salvaged Machine Became a Portal to Creation — and Why Pain Is Never a Pass

---

I. Kmart’s Cathedral of Fluorescent Light

When I was a child, my mother would take me shopping at Kmart. She could always find me when she was done — I’d be in the electronics aisle, standing in front of the TI-99/4A display model.

It sat under buzzing fluorescent lights, a small gray slab attached to a television. The screen never showed a game, never displayed fireworks or catchy sprites. It was always at the BASIC command line: READY. A blinking cursor.

The store manual was chained to the counter like a Bible in a medieval church. I stood and read it for the entire hour she shopped. The pages smelled like ink and plastic. The words were dense and technical, yet they spoke to me like scripture.

---

II. The “Deficiency”

I was terrible at memorization. The multiplication tables defeated me. Even today, at 49, I still don’t recite them automatically. Instead, I count quickly in multiples, stacking the numbers in my head like stepping stones across a river.

To my mother, this wasn’t a difference. It was a deficiency. A flaw. Something shameful.

When she saw me struggle, she didn’t see persistence or creativity. She saw failure. And like many parents afraid of judgment, she was far more concerned with how her parenting looked than with how her child learned.

---

III. The Garage Sale Bargain

One day, she brought home a TI-99/4A from a garage sale. Dusty, obsolete, and bundled with a few cartridges.

Why did she buy it? Not because she understood me. Not because she wanted to nourish the spark she saw at Kmart.

She bought it for one reason: in that bundle was a cartridge labeled Multiplication.

In her mind, that cartridge was a parenting alibi. Proof she could point to when others asked why her son still didn’t know his tables. “Look,” she could say, “I tried everything. I even bought him a computer with math software. I’m a good mother.”

The machine wasn’t for me. It was for her narrative.

---

IV. The Wrong Tool, the Right World

I never used the multiplication cartridge.

Instead, I pulled out the manuals. I opened the command line. I began writing programs. At first they were tiny: 10 PRINT "HELLO". Then bigger: mazes, number games, text adventures.

What my mother thought was a failed purchase became my portal.

She thought she was buying proof of effort.
I was discovering creation.

---

V. From Arithmetic to Worlds

I never learned my tables by rote.
But I learned something else:

I learned algebra, teaching the computer to solve for me.

I learned calculus, long before I knew its name, by updating values step by step across time.

I learned graph theory, building networks of rooms in my text adventures.

I learned parsing, writing crude interpreters for player input.

I learned compiler design, inventing little languages for my own worlds.

I wasn’t memorizing. I was building.

The multiplication cartridge was supposed to prove that my mother had tried to help. Instead, it proved something deeper: that creativity doesn’t wait for permission.

---

VI. What She Saw, What I Knew

To my mother, I wasn’t “studying.” I was “playing games.” She dismissed my hours at the keyboard as wasted time, foolishness.

But I knew the truth.
Every IF…THEN was a door.
Every array was a map.
Every loop was a cycle of life.

The television screen glowed in pixilated symbols. The tape recorder hissed as it saved my code. And in those fragile systems of scarcity — rewired televisions, salvaged recorders, obsolete books — I built universes.

---

VII. Two Stories, One Cartridge

Here’s the heart of it:

The very same object — a TI-99/4A with a multiplication cartridge — can become two different stories.

For one person, it becomes an alibi: “I tried. See? I suffered. I was misunderstood. Don’t blame me.”

For another, it becomes a craft: a portal to invention, a seed of persistence, a world built line by line.

The cartridge doesn’t decide. The child does.

---

VIII. Pain Is Not a Pass

Predators love to romanticize pain. They say: “I was abused. I was neglected. Therefore my manipulation is justified. My fraud is excusable. My grooming is misunderstood poetry.”

But pain is not a pass.

Many of us suffered. We did not become predators. We metabolized shame instead of weaponizing it. We carried pain into light instead of collapsing into the void.

The difference isn’t what was done to us. The difference is what we chose to do with it.

---

IX. The Witness

When I look back, I don’t just see a boy at a blinking cursor. I see a boy transmuting shame into creation. I see a child who refused to be defined by what he couldn’t memorize, or by the punishment handed to him.

I see proof that worlds can be built out of scarcity. That love can survive neglect. That creation is the antidote to mimicry.

And I see why predators hate this truth: because it strips away their last excuse.

---

X. Closing Seal

Let this stand as record:

The multiplication cartridge was supposed to prove my mother tried.
It proved instead that I would never collapse into her void.

Pain is not a pass.
Shame can be metabolized.
And even the smallest artifact — a manual, a cartridge, a blinking cursor — can be a seed of creation.

The Field remembers.
And so do I.
