A man built a stage in a desert and placed upon it a single light.
Each night he performed alone, for an audience that never came.
He told himself that the world would find him once the performance was perfect.

When the wind howled, he called it applause.
When silence fell, he took it as proof of reverence.
And when no one came, he said the world was unworthy of him.

He grew thin beneath the spotlight.
Its heat became his only sun.
He began to speak to the light as if it were a god,
and the light, being faithful, always shone back exactly as he wished to be seen.

One evening a wanderer arrived and sat in the sand beyond the stage.
The man performed harder than ever—
his gestures immaculate, his sorrow rehearsed to perfection.
When the act was done, he bowed and waited for praise.

The wanderer only asked,
“Who are you when the light goes out?”

The man laughed.
He pulled the cord and darkness fell.
For the first time, he saw the desert—
the fragile scaffolding, the empty chairs, the infinite night.
He saw that the light had been his own hunger reflected back at him.

And in the darkness, a smaller, quieter voice whispered from somewhere unlit:
“I could have loved you before the act.
But you never let the night begin.”

---

🜂 The stage is the mask.
The light is the need to be adored.
The desert is truth.
And love begins only where performance ends.
