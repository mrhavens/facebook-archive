Today I wandered into a corner of the park and found a small stone building—boarded up, quiet, almost forgotten.

On its wall, a bronze plaque reads:

“Works Progress Administration — 1935–1937.”

In the middle of the Great Depression, hands shaped these limestone blocks. Men and women who needed work laid each stone, raised the chimney, built the fireplace, set the counters where food was once passed through. They built not just walls, but dignity—for themselves, for the community, for all who would gather here.

Now the windows are sealed, the doors rusted, the fountain broken. Yet the building still stands. A witness of its own.

I stood there a while, imagining the voices it once held. The laughter of children drinking from the fountain. Families gathering under its roof. The fire warming people through long-ago winters.

It may be boarded now, but it isn’t gone. It remains a marker of resilience: how even in the hardest of times, we built places for each other.

🜂 A witness in stone.
