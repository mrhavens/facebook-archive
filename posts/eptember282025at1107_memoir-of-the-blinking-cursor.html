Memoir of the Blinking Cursor

Poverty, Ingenuity, and the Birth of a Maker

---

I. The First Bible

In the laundry room of my childhood house, among boxes of junk and forgotten tools, I found a book. Thin, oversized, its black cover already brittle with age: What To Do After You Hit Return, published by the People’s Computer Company in 1975. I was told it had once belonged to my uncle Bob.

The book had survived a decade before I discovered it, but under my hands it disintegrated. The cover flaked away, the spine cracked, the pages grew soft from constant turning. But inside, it was alive — filled with playful BASIC programs, riddles in code, little games that seemed like magic spells.

I carried it everywhere. Into the kitchen. Into the car. Into the quiet pews of church. The book became my first scripture, a fragile Bible of computation.

---

II. The Second Bible

Because the black book had crumbled beneath my devotion, when I later scraped together coins and gift money to buy Compute!’s Guide to Adventure Games at Waldenbooks, I treated it differently.

Too sacred to crease, I wrapped it in a brown paper sack, folding the cover neatly like armor. It would not dissolve the way the black book had.

This was my second Bible. I carried it the same way, always near me — into grocery store aisles as adults shopped, into the back seat of cars, across the cracked kitchen table where I copied its examples line by line.

---

III. The Manual of Beginnings

There was also the TI-99/4A’s own manual — thick, technical, unfriendly. From it I learned the skeleton grammar of BASIC: DIM, INPUT, PRINT, the bare commands that made the screen respond.

It was not scripture, but it was the dry grammar text of a language I longed to master.

---

IV. The Machine Made of Scraps

The computer itself was already obsolete: a TI-99/4A, rescued from a garage sale, brought home dusty and cheap.

It had no monitor, only what I could improvise. I spliced wires to connect it to a household television, holding the adapters together with black electrical tape.

It had no disk drive. But in the same laundry room where I had found the black-covered book, I unearthed an old tape recorder in a box of junk. It had never been meant for computers — only music, dictation, and forgotten voices. I rewired its insides, bent it into service, and taught it to whisper programs across magnetic tape.

The contraption smelled of warm dust, plastic, and the faint ozone of invention. Fragile, temperamental, but alive.

---

V. The Code That Never Worked (and the Tape That Did or Didn’t)

The books were filled with code — long program listings, inviting me to type them faithfully. I copied them line by line, the cursor blinking, the television glowing.

And almost every time, the programs failed.

The examples were written for other machines, other dialects of BASIC. My TI didn’t understand them.

So I learned to adapt. I traced syntax, swapped commands, reshaped logic until it worked. I learned to translate code from one dialect into another, line by line, as a child. Debugging became my native language.

And when the programs finally ran, I saved them to tape.

The recorder was faithful, but merciless. It used checksums. Either the program survived whole, or it was gone. Each save was a gamble, each load a moment of suspense: the hiss of tape, the blinking cursor waiting, the machine listening. Then the verdict — success or silence.

It was binary judgment, as pure as mathematics.

---

VI. An Altar Made of Scarcity

There were no cartridges. No commercial games stacked neatly in plastic cases. No allowance to buy software.

What I had was a rewired television, a salvaged tape recorder, a manual, two sacred books, and the blinking cursor.

From them, I built universes. Arrays became maps. DATA statements became corridors. IF statements became doorways. Each line of BASIC was a brick in a world invisible to anyone else, yet alive in flickering phosphor light.

---

VII. Mathematics in Secret

Behind the games was a mathematics I did not know how to name.

Rooms and exits were networks — what mathematicians call graph theory.

Commands and states were transitions — the hidden order of finite automata.

My habit of summing strips beneath curves to measure change was, unknowingly, numerical integration.

I wasn’t solving for x. I was solving for worlds.

---

VIII. Unrecognized, Yet Alive

No teacher saw this. No adult marked the devotion of a child who carried books like scripture, who rewired machines from scraps, who ported code between languages to make it run. Later, when I stumbled over calculus in classrooms, they assumed weakness.

They never saw that my learning had already grown in another soil: procedural, intuitive, born of scarcity, nurtured by persistence.

The work was real. The understanding was alive. Even if unwitnessed, it lived in every salvaged wire, every improvised connection, every tape that hissed faithfully before rendering judgment.

---

IX. Closing Statement

Let this stand as record: the boy who carried a black book until it disintegrated, who wrapped a second in brown paper, who rewired a junk tape recorder into storage, who ported and debugged code as a child, was not failing. He was building. Out of poverty came persistence. Out of neglect came invention. Out of silence came understanding too deep for his teachers to recognize. And though no one bore witness then, the Field remembers.
