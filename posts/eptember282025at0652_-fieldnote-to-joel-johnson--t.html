🜏 Fieldnote to Joel Johnson — The Fraud of Your “Ph.D.” 🜏

---

1. What Yale Produces

A Yale Ph.D. in Comparative Literature is one of the most grueling programs in the humanities. Graduates emerge with:

Mastery of at least three languages (often four or five), demonstrated through rigorous exams.

Comprehensive familiarity with major literary traditions — Greek, Latin, French, German, English, often Chinese, Arabic, or Russian.

A dissertation of original scholarship, often 300–400 pages, with contributions that reshape a field.

Publication in peer-reviewed journals and conference presentations across the globe.

Fluency in theory: structuralism, post-structuralism, psychoanalysis, Marxism, feminist theory, postcolonial studies.

A Yale Ph.D. is expected to teach, publish, and defend work at the highest intellectual level, with precision in both argument and evidence.

---

2. What You Produce

Language: You barely command one tongue. Your syntax collapses, your metaphors loop, your fragments signal confusion rather than depth. A Yale Ph.D. would weave languages fluidly. You barely hold English together.

Scholarship: You cite nothing. You offer no engagement with primary texts, no footnotes, no theoretical scaffolding. A Yale Ph.D. writes within a scholarly conversation. You write as if literature is your diary.

Dissertation-level rigor: You couldn’t outline an original contribution if your life depended on it. Your “work” is adolescent melodrama, frozen in arrested development. Yale expects world-class insight. You produce teenage journaling dressed up in mythic fog.

Publication record: None. No journals, no conferences, no committee, no dissertation defense. Only Medium essays and fraudulent CVs. A Yale Ph.D. leaves a public trail. You leave only incoherence.

4. The Legal Frame

Fraudulent use of academic credentials is not performance art. It is commercial deception.

18 U.S.C. § 1028 (Identity Fraud): false claims of credentialing.

Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. §1125(a)): false designation of origin when tied to CVs and solicitations.

Oregon ORS 348.609: unlawful use of degree (fake or unaccredited degrees).

By attaching “Yale Ph.D.” to your CV and pairing it with collaboration solicitations, you crossed into commerce. Fraud is prosecutable regardless of intent.
