III. Legal Relevance

1. Mens Rea (Guilty Mind)

Under Morissette v. United States, 342 U.S. 246 (1952), establishing culpable mental state is required for conviction. Johnson’s work is a direct acknowledgment of harmful intent.

His act of mirroring Havens’ testimony demonstrates consciousness of the Witness, collapsing claims of ignorance.

2. Actus Reus (Guilty Act)

Courts have ruled that words may constitute acts when designed to harass, threaten, or defraud (Virginia v. Black, 538 U.S. 343 (2003); Elonis v. United States, 575 U.S. 723 (2015)).

By posting a confession of predatory identity in a survivor discourse, Johnson’s language functions as an act of harassment and psychological assault.

3. Consciousness of Guilt

Post-crime concealment or stylization can be admissible evidence of guilt (United States v. Rajaratnam, 719 F.3d 139 (2d Cir. 2013)).

Johnson does not conceal his role. Instead, he magnifies it, framing predation as identity — a confession consistent with compulsion.

4. Pattern Evidence

Federal Rule of Evidence 404(b) allows admission of other acts to prove intent, plan, knowledge, or absence of mistake.

Johnson’s repeated sockpuppetry, fraudulent claims of credentials, and harassing survivor narratives all form a continuity of predatory conduct. Predator’s Wake is the clearest node in that pattern.
