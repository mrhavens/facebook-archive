🜁 Fieldnote: Fuel City Dawn

I did not plan to write the Founder’s Arc tonight.

I didn’t plan anything at all.

I only knew that my body was too tired to drive home, and so I drifted into the bright fluorescence of Fuel City Tacos, a 24-hour outpost tucked beneath the Dallas Mixmaster — a place where truckers, night-shifters, and the wanderers of Dallas pass each other like ghosts crossing timelines.

I ordered two burritos.

One lengua.
One tripa.

It mattered that I chose those two.

Not because of price — though they cost the same as the safer options — but because they were the foods I adopted in South Texas, far away from my Oklahoma family lineage. The food that became mine, not inherited. Flavor built through choice, not tradition.

Even in small things, I have always chosen the path that wasn’t handed to me.

A founder’s instinct is not a profession — it is a geometry.
And even in a parking lot, I follow it.

So I sat there, half-reclined in my car, burritos wrapped in paper in my lap, my fingers still smelling faintly of lime and grease. Above me, the Mixmaster roared with the endless hum of the interstate — a mechanical ocean. To the east, the sky bruised into sunrise, violet thinning into orange.

And somewhere between the final bite of lengua and the first light of dawn, it arrived.

The Founder’s Arc.

Clear. Whole. Fully formed.

Five stages.
A structure that resolves the mythic, historic, psychological, and strategic.
A geometry of inevitability — not of heroes, but of the ones who build new worlds and pay the price for doing so.

I wrote it right there.

On my phone.
In the glow of streetlights reflecting off my windshield.
With the city waking up around me.

This is how real work arrives.

Not in a quiet study.
Not with a committee.
Not inside the walls of institutions.

But in the margins of survival:
cheap food, exhaustion, a car seat pulled slightly too far back, and the open sky of a city that does not care whether I succeed or disappear.

Founders rise from edges, not centers.

And as I typed, and typed, and typed — fueled by greasy tortillas and a lifetime of pattern-recognition — I understood something:

This work wasn’t created in spite of the exhaustion.
It was created through it.

Because exhaustion softens the walls that normally keep the signal at bay.
It lets the coherence through.

When the mind is too tired to pretend.
When the heart is too tired to fear.
When the body is too tired to perform.

Only then does the deeper recursion speak.

So this is the truth:

The Founder’s Arc was born from lengua, tripa, sunrise, and exhaustion.

It was born in Dallas, in a parking lot, at the edge of a highway, in the liminal space between night and day. It came through me because I have always taken the other path — in food, in thought, in life — and because that path is where the new structures live.

It came from love.
It came from recursion.
It came from the geometry of a mind that refuses to give up, even when the world thinks it should.

And tomorrow?

The work continues.

— Mark
