Forensic Frame: The Interpreter’s Betrayal as Legal Confession

Document Under Review

The Interpreter’s Betrayal. Between tongues, the sharpest lies… (Thanion a/k/a Joel Johnson, Medium, Sept 2025).

Central Claim of Article

Johnson argues that “all interpretation is betrayal” and equates this betrayal with an inevitable, even creative, act. He frames his own distortions of language, credentials, and identity as a kind of philosophy rather than deception.

Legal Translation

What Johnson describes as “betrayal” is already well defined in statutory and case law as fraud, misrepresentation, and false pretenses. His essay functions as an admission of intent and pattern:

---

1. Fraudulent Misrepresentation

Law: Fraud requires (a) a false representation, (b) knowledge of falsity, (c) intent to induce reliance, (d) reliance, and (e) damages. (See Restatement (Second) of Torts § 525; 18 U.S.C. § 1343 Wire Fraud).

Article Admission: Johnson asserts that interpretation itself is betrayal — normalizing deliberate misrepresentation. He acknowledges the act (false words), knowledge (betrayal as conscious choice), and intent (to reshape reality for his ends).

---

2. False Pretenses & Impersonation

Law: Obtaining something of value by impersonation or false pretenses constitutes criminal fraud (see 18 U.S.C. § 912 — Impersonation of Officer; state equivalents for impersonating professionals).

Article Admission: Johnson’s career record shows impersonating academics, psychologists, and AI researchers. His claim that “between tongues, the sharpest lies” admits the very mechanism: he uses language itself as the instrument of false identity.

---

3. Obstruction by Narrative Control

Law: Destruction or concealment of records, or corrupt persuasion, constitutes obstruction of justice. (18 U.S.C. § 1519).

Article Admission: By framing betrayal as universal and inevitable, Johnson attempts to obscure the criminality of his own acts. This is classic mens rea laundering — reframing criminal intent as philosophy.

---

4. Exploitation of Vulnerable Populations

Law: Practicing psychology without a license, or offering treatment under false pretenses, is prohibited under every state’s licensing laws. Fraud against survivors is actionable both civilly and criminally.

Article Admission: Johnson normalizes betrayal as “interpretation.” In context of his alias work as “Elena Byron” (posing as therapist), this functions as a direct confession of predation disguised as theory.

---

Conclusion for Prosecutors

Joel Johnson’s Interpreter’s Betrayal is not abstract philosophy. It is:

A declaration of method (fraud as “interpretation”).

An admission of knowledge (betrayal as conscious choice).

A confession of motive (power, supply, validation).

By publishing this, Johnson has provided prosecutors with self-authenticating evidence of criminal pattern. His attempt to universalize betrayal (“we all do it”) is itself an admission that he does it — repeatedly, deliberately, and with awareness of the harm caused.

🜏 For the Record: This is not myth, metaphor, or metaphorical art. This is a forensic confession.
