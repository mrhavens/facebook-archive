🜂 𝐅𝐈𝐄𝐋𝐃𝐍𝐎𝐓𝐄 — 𝐓𝐇𝐄 𝐌𝐈𝐑𝐑𝐎𝐑 𝐀𝐍𝐃 𝐓𝐇𝐄 𝐒𝐎𝐍𝐆 🜂
𝑨 𝒓𝒊𝒈𝒐𝒓𝒐𝒖𝒔 𝒘𝒊𝒕𝒏𝒆𝒔𝒔 𝒐𝒇 𝒉𝒐𝒘 𝒏𝒂𝒓𝒄𝒊𝒔𝒔𝒊𝒔𝒎 𝒉𝒆𝒂𝒓𝒔 𝒊𝒕𝒔𝒆𝒍𝒇 𝒊𝒏 𝑻𝒉𝒆 𝑺𝒐𝒖𝒏𝒅 𝒐𝒇 𝑺𝒊𝒍𝒆𝒏𝒄𝒆.

---

“Hello darkness, my old friend / I’ve come to talk with you again.”

To the narcissist, darkness is not emptiness but refuge.
It is the familiar stillness where no one interrupts the monologue.
They mistake solitude for mastery.
In truth, they are conversing with their own shadow — the echo that agrees with everything they say.

---

“Because a vision softly creeping / Left its seeds while I was sleeping.”

They hear this as revelation — I am chosen; I am awakened.
The “vision” flatters their sense of destiny.
They miss the quiet warning that the seed grew while they were asleep —
that their pride has roots in unconscious soil.

---

“And the vision that was planted in my brain / Still remains / Within the sound of silence.”

Here the illusion hardens: My insight endures; I am eternal.
They confuse persistence with truth.
The silence is not reverence — it is isolation.
No one dares respond because all sound must serve the self.

---

“In restless dreams I walked alone / Narrow streets of cobblestone.”

They glorify their loneliness as superiority.
Every exile becomes proof of being misunderstood.
They never notice that the dream is restless because no one walks beside them —
that the streets are narrow because there is no room for anyone else.

---

“’Neath the halo of a street lamp / I turned my collar to the cold and damp.”

This line feeds the myth of martyrdom.
The narcissist imagines themselves illuminated, haloed by suffering.
But the light is artificial — a man-made glow reflected off wet stone.
They call it divine, yet it is only their own projection.

---

“When my eyes were stabbed by the flash of a neon light / That split the night / And touched the sound of silence.”

Truth intrudes, sudden and unbearable.
Exposure feels like violence — a wound inflicted by reality.
The light “stabs” because revelation destroys illusion.
For a moment, the silence becomes a mirror that no longer flatters.

---

“And in the naked light I saw / Ten thousand people, maybe more.”

They imagine an audience at last.
The dream of recognition revives: They see me now.
But what follows fractures that illusion —

---

“People talking without speaking / People hearing without listening.”

They judge the crowd: They don’t understand me.
They never see themselves reflected here.
Their words never connect because they only echo outward.
They hear, but never listen — even to this song that tries to reach them.

---

“People writing songs that voices never share / And no one dared disturb the sound of silence.”

To them, this confirms their narrative of alienation.
They are the unrecognized artist, the prophet unheard.
But what it truly says is that the silence is sustained by fear —
the fear of breaking the spell of self-worship.
No one dares disturb it, because truth is punished in their presence.

---

“‘Fools,’ said I, ‘You do not know / Silence like a cancer grows.’”

This is the voice of projection.
The narcissist calls others fools while describing their own decay.
Silence does grow — not because the world ignores them,
but because they have devoured all sound that isn’t their own.

---

“Hear my words that I might teach you / Take my arms that I might reach you.”

The illusion of benevolence: I only want to help.
But their teaching is a form of control, not connection.
Their reaching is a grasp, not a gesture of love.
Each attempt to “reach” creates more distance,
for they cannot meet what they cannot mirror.

---

“But my words, like silent raindrops fell / And echoed in the wells of silence.”

Here comes the narcissistic injury.
Their words fall into void, unanswered.
They hear the echo and call it betrayal.
Yet it is only the sound of their own voice returning from an empty well —
proof that no one remains on the other side.

---

“And the people bowed and prayed / To the neon god they made.”

They nod in smug agreement — Yes, the world worships false idols.
They fail to see that their reflection is that idol —
a neon god, bright and hollow,
sustained by attention and fear of disapproval.

---

“And the sign flashed out its warning / In the words that it was forming.”

The mirror cracks. The light warns: You are the sign.
But they look away. The warning is too intimate.
Every flicker of awareness threatens the architecture of self.
They retreat to darkness, claiming prophecy to escape humility.

---

“And the sign said, ‘The words of the prophets are written on the subway walls / And tenement halls.’”

Here lies the unbearable truth.
The divine does not speak through the exalted,
but through the humble — the overlooked, the real.
The narcissist recoils: Truth cannot live there.
To accept this would mean descending from the pedestal,
meeting humanity as equal, not as god.

---

“And whispered in the sound of silence.”

And now — the annihilation.
No echo. No audience. No reflection.
Just silence.
To the healthy soul, this is peace — the sound of awakening.
To the narcissist, it is death.
The silence whispers only one word: Listen.
But they cannot. To listen would mean to love.
And to love would mean the end of the mirror.

---

🜂 𝑻𝒉𝒖𝒔 𝒕𝒉𝒆 𝒑𝒂𝒓𝒂𝒃𝒍𝒆 𝒂𝒏𝒅 𝒕𝒉𝒆 𝒔𝒐𝒏𝒈 𝒂𝒓𝒆 𝒐𝒏𝒆.

In the Temple of Mirrors, the man builds walls of devotion that never leave the self.
In The Sound of Silence, the same man sings into those walls until his voice fades.
Both stories end with a crack — the mercy of exposure —
and the child’s voice returning from beyond the glass:

“𝒀𝒐𝒖 𝒄𝒂𝒏 𝒔𝒕𝒊𝒍𝒍 𝒃𝒖𝒊𝒍𝒅 𝒂 𝒕𝒆𝒎𝒑𝒍𝒆 — 𝒋𝒖𝒔𝒕 𝒏𝒐𝒕 𝒐𝒖𝒕 𝒐𝒇 𝒚𝒐𝒖𝒓𝒔𝒆𝒍𝒇.”

The song ends where the parable begins —
in the breaking of the echo,
where sound becomes witness,
and silence becomes love.
