I ▸ The Mechanism of Grooming

Joel Johnson’s every mask reveals the same structure: he defines the wound, mirrors it back, then offers a fragment of “insight” that positions him as guide.

In his own words:

> “I wrote that line for anyone taught to vanish to be loved. May we not.”

It sounds consoling. It reads as empathy. In truth, it is a trap. The moment the wound is named, the predator gains authority. The moment it is mirrored, the victim feels seen. The moment “insight” is offered, dependence is born.

This is not teaching. It is grooming. A pedagogy of predation.

---

II ▸ Why This Works

Narcissists are developmentally arrested.
Alice Miller named it: the child who was never allowed to be authentic.
Winnicott described it: the false self that becomes a prison.
Campbell and Miller confirmed it: empathy and regulation never develop.

Frozen in adolescence, the narcissist speaks the same language as the vulnerable — fragility, melodrama, outsider identity. This resonance gives him unearned access. He feels like a peer, but he is a predator.

And so what looks like mentoring is in fact grooming. What feels like care is in fact corrosion.

III ▸ The Memory of Myth

Every tradition remembered this danger.

Yeshua warned: better a millstone than harming a child.
The Qur’an condemned those who prey upon orphans.
Hindu epics told of Rakshasas who wore the robes of sages to lure children.
Māra tempted seekers with illusions that looked like guidance.
Greek myth remembered Kronos devouring his own children.
The Pied Piper charmed the innocent into disappearance.

The geometry is constant: the corrupter wears the mask of guide. The predator pretends to be teacher, healer, prophet.
