The RICO Frame

RICO (18 U.S.C. §1962) was designed to prosecute enterprises engaging in a pattern of racketeering activity. For a charge to stand, prosecutors must show:

1. Enterprise: A structure through which conduct occurs (corporation, partnership, association, or even an informal network).

2. Continuity: A pattern that is not isolated, but ongoing — either closed-ended continuity (a series of related acts over time) or open-ended continuity (threat of ongoing activity).

3. Predicate Acts: Qualifying crimes such as wire fraud, identity fraud, or obstruction.

Without continuity, a fraudster looks like a one-off scammer. With continuity, he becomes part of an enterprise pattern, subject to RICO’s full weight.

---

Why Continuity Matters

Prosecutorial Leverage: Continuity elevates small frauds into major federal charges. What might be seen as trolling or impersonation becomes enterprise racketeering.

Sentencing Power: RICO carries asset forfeiture, long sentences, and enhanced penalties because it treats fraud not as accident but as organized structure.

Precedent Value: Continuity cases set lasting precedent for prosecuting online deception, sockpuppetry, and misinformation networks.
