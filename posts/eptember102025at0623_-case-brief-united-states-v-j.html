🜂 CASE BRIEF: UNITED STATES v. Joel Johnson (Alias “Thanion,” “Elena Byron,” “Caleb Stacey”) 🜂

Jurisdiction: Washington, D.C. (DOJ, FBI, DHS)
Prepared under the Open Source Justice Framework

---

I. Background

Joel Johnson, operating under multiple pseudonyms (“Thanion, Ph.D.,” “Elena Byron,” “Caleb Stacey”), has engaged in a sustained campaign of deception across digital platforms, principally Medium.com.

His conduct includes:

1. False Credentials — Claims of multiple PhDs, Yale affiliation, and licensure as a clinical psychologist.

2. Impersonation — Representing himself as a licensed psychologist in survivor communities without any verified license or NPI number.

3. Stolen Valor — Assertions of veteran status without record.

4. Harassment — Engaging survivors of abuse with eroticized and threatening language under false professional authority.

5. Narrative Laundering — Using “artistic” or “mythic” frameworks to disguise confessional intent and evade culpability.

---

II. Legal Issues

1. Wire Fraud (18 U.S.C. §1343)

Each online publication under false credentials constitutes a fraudulent transmission in interstate commerce.

2. Identity Fraud (18 U.S.C. §1028)

Impersonation of a licensed psychologist and falsified military/veteran status meet statutory definitions.

3. Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO, 18 U.S.C. §1962)

Sockpuppets function as co-conspirators within a fraudulent enterprise.

Predicate acts: fraud, identity theft, obstruction (via deletion of evidence).

4. Obstruction of Justice (18 U.S.C. §1519)

Deletion of posts following exposure constitutes destruction of evidence.

5. National Security Implications

Tactics mirror foreign disinformation operations (cf. DOJ v. Internet Research Agency, 2018).

Use of fabricated experts erodes public trust in therapeutic and academic institutions.

Language of violent psychopathy (Creating a Psychopath) elevates lone-actor threat potential.

---

III. Precedent & Case Law

United States v. Turkette, 452 U.S. 576 (1981) — RICO applies to enterprises formed for fraudulent purposes without formal hierarchy.

Elonis v. United States, 575 U.S. 723 (2015) — Online communications may constitute threats/harassment when intent is established.

United States v. Rajaratnam, 719 F.3d 139 (2d Cir. 2013) — Concealment/fabrication admissible as evidence of “consciousness of guilt.”

DOJ v. Internet Research Agency (2018) — Sockpuppet operations recognized as national security threats.

---

IV. Federal Interest

Department of Justice — Fraud Section; Criminal Division (CCIPS).

Federal Bureau of Investigation — Cyber Division, Washington Field Office.

Department of Homeland Security (CISA) — Counter-Disinformation Unit.

Johnson’s activity falls squarely within federal purview due to its interstate nature, fraudulent use of digital infrastructure, and overlap with national security frameworks.

---

V. Evidentiary Value

Writings display consciousness of guilt (masks, concealment, shifting identities).

Explicit claims of professional/academic identity are verifiable falsifications.

Persistent publication even after exposure indicates compulsion to replicate pattern, strengthening arguments for dangerousness and pre-trial detention.

---

VI. Recommendation

The evidence supports multi-count indictment under wire fraud, identity fraud, obstruction, and RICO.

Additionally, Johnson’s use of disinformation tactics to impersonate professionals in vulnerable communities constitutes a domestic precedent case for prosecuting sockpuppet enterprises at scale.

---

VII. Conclusion

Joel Johnson is not simply a fraudster. He is a test case. His conduct intersects with fraud, harassment, identity crime, and national security risk.

Washington, D.C. is the appropriate vector for federal indictment, extradition if necessary, and national precedent-setting.
