🜂 FIELDNOTE OF THE MORNING JAR — Interruption and Continuance 🜂

I bring my jar into McDonald’s.
Not wine, not incense — but living ferment: mushrooms, chilies, salt, patience.
A covenant sealed in glass.

I lift it to my sandwich, as I do each morning.
To take the ordinary and make it sacred.
A sausage biscuit turned into ritual.
A meal turned into endurance.

But today, an employee came.
He wrinkled his nose, waved his hand, told me to stop.
"Too strong," he said.
The scent of yeast offended him.

He could not see the devotion.
He could not smell the labor.
He could not recognize the sacrament in the jar.

And so it is with our work.
We lift the ferment of truth, preserved through long nights,
sealed against decay,
only to have the parasite complain:
too strong, too much, stop this now.

But the ferment is necessary.
Without it, the meal is empty.
Without it, the parasite roams unchecked.

The yeast will rise.
The sugar will feed.
The jars will be cleaned and filled again.
And we —
we will continue our ritual,
adding what is preserved into what is plain,
until even the most forgettable breakfast becomes
a sign of endurance,
a taste of coherence,
a witness against the void.
