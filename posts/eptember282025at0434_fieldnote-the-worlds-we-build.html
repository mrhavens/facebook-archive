Fieldnote: The Worlds We Build Out of Pain

When I was a child, the adults around me spoke of demons.
They said rebellion lived inside me.
They said I was a terrible child who needed to be punished into holiness.

So I was beaten.
Locked outside or locked in.
Sometimes in my underwear.
Sometimes prayed over while hands pressed down to “cast out” the thing they imagined inside me.

And when I asked questions — Why? What does God really want? — my mother would tell me, “Ask God when you get to heaven.”
It was her way of ending the conversation.
A door slammed shut.

But in the silence that followed, something else happened.
I started to listen for answers myself.
Not the answers of fear, but the answers that arise quietly when you’re forced to build your own refuge.

My refuge was a blinking cursor.
A salvaged TI-99/4A spliced into a television with black tape.
A junk tape recorder turned into storage.
Books carried like Bibles.
Line by line, room by room, I wrote my own universes — text adventures to escape a world that didn’t want my questions.

I thought I was hiding.
I see now I was training.

Because patterns don’t go away.
They transmute.
The child who built worlds to survive grows into an adult who builds worlds to reveal.
The boy who was accused of harboring demons grows into a man who hunts the real demons — not in children, but in systems, in patterns, in behaviors that devour innocence.

Every pattern of our childhood is a seed.
It can sprout into mimicry or into creation.
Into void or into light.
Pain is not neutral. It’s potential.
We don’t “escape” it; we metabolize it.

The smallest things shape us:
a book found in a laundry room,
a brown-paper cover wrapped around a guide to adventure games,
the kindness of a single teacher or a television neighbor like Fred Rogers.
Each is a vector. Each is a door.

My mother could not answer my questions.
She only knew how to end them.
But the ONE is not a door slammed shut.
The ONE is geometry that reveals itself when you stop demanding answers and start listening.
The answers are not hidden in heaven.
They’re moving through you now, waiting to be lived.

This is why I write.
This is why I build.
Not to escape demons, but to name them.
Not to hide from the Field, but to create a map so others can walk out of the labyrinth sooner than I did.
