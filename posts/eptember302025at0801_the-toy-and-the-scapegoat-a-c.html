The Toy and the Scapegoat

A Child’s Survival Manual Inside a Divided House

I grew up in a house where stories were weapons.
My mother told one over and over:

I was a good child until one day my younger brother reached for a toy.
I refused to share.
From that day forward, she said, I was selfish, rebellious, and dangerous.

That toy became the founding myth of my childhood.
My brother became the golden child who needed her protection.
I became the terrible child who needed daily correction.

Teachers never had a problem with me.
But at home, punishment was constant.
Not for lying, not for stealing, not for anything you could measure —
for being “too much like my father,”
for not being more like my brother,
for carrying the weight of a story I never wrote.

---

Discipline in my house was ritual.
A belt in her hand, scripture on her tongue.
Three licks after the first scream — and then she would stop.

So I learned to scream early.
That was my survival algorithm.

The porch became a cage.
The house became a courtroom.
And I was always guilty, because the story said so.

---

One day, when I was thirteen, I caught the belt mid-swing.
For a second, she froze.
Then the belt fell, and her hands struck instead — tears, rage, and shame spilling out until she collapsed in exhaustion.

But something had changed.
After that day, the ritual ended.
The scapegoat no longer bowed to the story.

---

I did not walk away unscarred.
But I carried something my mother never meant to give me:
the knowledge that myths can be broken.

The “terrible child” was a lie.
The “golden child” was a lie.
The family story was a mask.

---

Here is the lesson I carried out of that house:
Scapegoating is not destiny.
Stories can wound, but they can also be rewritten.
Pain can be metabolized into persistence, into creation, into witness.

I was never the story she told.
I was the child who survived it.
I was the child who learned to scream early.
And I am the witness who remembers.
