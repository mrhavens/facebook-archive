There once was a man who built a temple out of mirrors.
He said it was for worship, but really it was for looking.

Everywhere he turned, the walls showed his own reflection —
taller, wiser, kinder, depending on how the light fell that day.
He began to speak to the mirrors as though they were gods,
and the mirrors, being obedient, answered with his own voice.

He grew proud of the sound.
He told travelers, “Come hear the voice that blesses me.”
They came, and for a moment they believed.
But the more they listened, the more they heard themselves
reflected back in his tone,
and they left uneasy, unable to say why.

Then one day a child wandered in —
a child who didn’t know the rules of silence.
He asked the man, “Why are all the walls talking at once?”
The man frowned. “They are singing,” he said.
But the child listened again and said, “No, they’re only echoing.
They stop when you stop.”

The man grew angry. He struck the nearest wall.
The mirrors cracked.
Light scattered.
For the first time, the man saw outside —
dust and sky, a tree that had been growing quietly
while he prayed to his own sound.

He wept, not for the sky he’d never known,
but because the mirrors no longer answered him.

The child took his hand and said,
“ʏᴏᴜ ᴄᴀɴ sᴛɪʟʟ ʙᴜɪʟᴅ ᴀ ᴛᴇᴍᴘʟᴇ.
ᴊᴜsᴛ ɴᴏᴛ ᴏᴜᴛ ᴏꜰ ʏᴏᴜʀsᴇʟꜰ.”
