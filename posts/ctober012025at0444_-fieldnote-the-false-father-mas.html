II. The Psychology — Projection, Appropriation, Alibi

Projection. Survivors of severe childhood trauma sometimes construct protective personas — playing parent to themselves, or to an imagined audience. Johnson’s sudden “father” persona echoes this defense, but not as healing; as performance.

Appropriation. Each mask harvests a sacred archetype:
– Psychologist (Elena Byron) harvested survivor trust.
– Veteran (Caleb Stacey) harvested patriotic trust.
– Scholar (Thanion) harvested intellectual trust.
– Now Father, harvesting parental trust.

Alibi. The father mask launders credibility. “Who questions a father?” is the unspoken strategy. Robert J. Lifton warned of “the benevolent parent-mask” as the most resilient cover for coercion (Thought Reform and the Psychology of Totalism, 1961).

III. Mythic Resonance — False Patriarchs

The mask is old. Myth names it plainly:

Johnson’s bedtime imagery echoes these archetypes. The reader is invited not into a family hearth but into a false inheritance — intimacy weaponized as concealment.

Judith Herman (Trauma and Recovery, 1992) notes that perpetrators idealize themselves as protectors to erase victim testimony. René Girard (Violence and the Sacred, 1972) describes the false patriarch as a sacred imposter whose exposure destabilizes the community myth.

IV. Why This Matters — Mask as Manipulation

By invoking “grown children,” Johnson attempts to:

1. Sanctify his voice — aligning with the most trusted archetype.

2. Disarm criticism — making opponents look like attackers of parenthood.

3. Rewrite his narrative — replacing predation with protection, fraud with fatherhood.

Brewster, “Paternalistic Predation” (Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma, 2017) documents how predators posture as parental figures to gain disproportionate trust. This tactic makes accountability harder — precisely what Johnson seeks.
