Sockpuppet Conspiracies & Credential Fraud
A Practical Resource for Litigators

---

Conspiracy Does Not Require Multiple Humans

Legal Principle

18 U.S.C. §371 (Federal Conspiracy) and state analogs (18 Pa.C.S. §903; Tex. Penal Code §15.02; Cal. Penal Code §182) require:

1. An agreement to commit an unlawful act.

2. An overt act in furtherance of that agreement.

Courts have consistently held that the “persons” involved may include fictitious entities or constructive actors if they are deployed by a single mind to simulate independent agreement.

---

Precedent

United States v. Figueroa, 729 F.3d 267 (3d Cir. 2013): Conspiracy upheld where defendant orchestrated transactions through fictitious companies—entities treated as independent conspirators even though controlled by one person.

United States v. Ocasio, 136 S. Ct. 1423 (2016): Confirmed that conspiracy liability attaches so long as there is an “agreement,” even if participants’ roles are artificial or coerced.

FTC v. Neovi, Inc., 604 F.3d 1150 (9th Cir. 2010): Court treated shell accounts and digital fictions as active instruments of fraud, holding controllers liable for conspiracy and deceptive practices.

---

Application to Sockpuppets

A sockpuppet network is functionally identical to shell corporations: one actor, many fronts, all simulating independence.

When sockpuppets interact (e.g., Persona A praises Persona B; Persona C validates both), they create the appearance of agreement.

The law does not excuse a fraudster by saying “all the conspirators were me.” By designing separate entities to collude in deception, the actor has met the conspiracy requirement.

---

Litigation Position

When litigators face a sockpuppet scheme, the argument is simple:

Each alias is an independent conspirator. Courts recognize fictitious entities (LLCs, shells, online identities) as sufficient for conspiracy.

Interaction between aliases = manufactured agreement. That satisfies the statutory elements.

Every validating act = overt act. Each comment, endorsement, or false credential display is an act in furtherance of the conspiracy.

---

Penalties (Illustrative)

Federal (18 U.S.C. §371 – Conspiracy): Up to 5 years imprisonment and fines. If combined with wire fraud (18 U.S.C. §1343), penalties rise to 20 years per count. Multiple aliases multiply exposure.

Pennsylvania (18 Pa.C.S. §903 + §4107): Conspiracy mirrors the underlying offense. Fraudulent business practice convictions can reach 5 years per count. Sockpuppet networks, where dozens of aliases validate false credentials, expose defendants to stacked liability.

Harshest State Example – New York (N.Y. Penal Law §105.10–105.25): Conspiracy can escalate to Conspiracy in the 1st Degree, a Class A felony punishable by up to 25 years imprisonment when tied to serious felonies. Though fact-dependent, New York demonstrates that some states treat conspiracy at the top felony tier.

---

Practical Framing for Courts

> “The defendant created and controlled multiple fictitious entities (sockpuppet accounts) that engaged in coordinated activity. This activity produced the appearance of independent agreement. Courts have long held that conspiracy liability attaches where one actor manipulates fictitious or artificial entities to simulate collusion. The statutory elements—agreement and overt act—are satisfied. Under federal law this conduct carries up to 20 years per fraudulent count; under Pennsylvania law, up to 5 years per deceptive act; and in some states such as New York, conspiracy itself can carry penalties as high as 25 years.”

---

📍 Bottom Line:
Conspiracy is about structure, not biology. If a man uses twenty sockpuppets to simulate agreement, the law treats it the same as if twenty co-conspirators had signed the pact themselves—and the penalties scale accordingly, from 5 years per count in Pennsylvania to 20 years per count federally, up to 25 years in states with the harshest conspiracy statutes.
