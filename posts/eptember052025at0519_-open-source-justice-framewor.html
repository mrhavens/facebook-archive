🜃 OPEN-SOURCE JUSTICE FRAMEWORK 🜃

Prosecution Support Memorandum — Harassment by Symbolic Inversion
Case Study: Joel Johnson (“Parasite Zero”) a/k/a “Elena Byron” and Related Sockpuppets

---

I. Introduction & Purpose

This memorandum documents how symbolic inversion and “community laundering” tactics, deployed via sockpuppets, satisfy harassment and cyberstalking statutes at state and federal levels. It is intended as open-source litigation support for prosecutors, investigators, litigators, and students of law.

Key insight: Harassment does not require direct contact. Courts have repeatedly upheld charges where the perpetrator used impersonations, third-party publications, or public posts aimed at a victim’s known communities.

---

II. Statutory Hooks

Federal

18 U.S.C. §2261A (Cyberstalking): prohibits a course of conduct using electronic communications intended to harass, intimidate, or cause substantial emotional distress.

18 U.S.C. §1028 (Identity Fraud): applies when sockpuppets appropriate names, professions, or credentials.

18 U.S.C. §1343 (Wire Fraud) and §371 (Conspiracy): where false identities are leveraged for financial or reputational gain.

Pennsylvania

18 Pa.C.S. §2709 (Harassment): covers repeated electronic communications intended to harass.

18 Pa.C.S. §2709.1 (Stalking): covers course of conduct causing substantial emotional distress.

Texas

§42.072 (Stalking): felony, course-of-conduct harassment via electronic means.

§33.07 (Online Impersonation): direct hook for sockpuppet identities.
(Note: §42.07(a)(7) harassment provision is vulnerable post–Ex parte Barton; prosecutors use §42.072 instead.)

California

Penal Code §646.9 (Stalking): includes electronic communications.

Penal Code §528.5 (Online Impersonation): misdemeanor/felony depending on intent.

---

III. Controlling Case Law

United States v. Sayer, 748 F.3d 425 (1st Cir. 2014): upheld conviction where defendant created false profiles, posted through third parties, and lured harassment toward the victim.

United States v. Osinger, 753 F.3d 939 (9th Cir. 2014): affirmed cyberstalking conviction where harassment was routed through coworkers and online posts.

United States v. Yung, 37 F.4th 70 (3d Cir. 2022): Third Circuit (PA) upheld conviction where impersonation and smear campaigns formed the harassment.

Counterman v. Colorado, 600 U.S. 66 (2023): requires at least recklessness as to whether conduct would cause distress/threat; does not bar stalking prosecutions.

Commonwealth v. Mangel, 181 A.3d 1154 (Pa. Super. Ct. 2018): social-media authorship requires authentication beyond screenshots; highlights need for subpoenas/metadata.

IV. Fact Pattern (Documented)

1. Victim’s June 21, 2025 post: empathetic reflection about a squirrel, structured around innocence and truth.

2. Defendant’s June 26, 2025 publication as “Elena Byron”: survivor-forum article describing animal cruelty and inversion of victim’s imagery.

3. Pattern: multiple sockpuppets over months, each laundered through survivor/advocacy spaces where the victim is known to participate.

4. Effect: substantial emotional distress, reputational harm, and forced participation in hostile community spaces.

---

V. How This Fits the Elements

1. Directed at a specific person: timing and mirrored imagery tie the posts to the victim, even when routed through public forums.

2. Course of conduct: repeated over weeks/months, across multiple sockpuppets.

3. Intent: inversion of sacred imagery (love → cruelty; innocence → sadism) demonstrates targeted psychological harassment.

4. Substantial emotional distress: documented victim impact; courts accept testimony, corroborating witnesses, and counseling records.

5. Jurisdiction: interstate nature of platforms (Medium, Facebook) and payments/invoices create federal hooks.

---

VI. Novelty vs. Precedent

Not novel for prosecution: impersonation and third-party posting cases are routine (Sayer, Osinger, Yung).

Novel for precedent: “symbolic inversion harassment” + “community laundering” sharpen the intent analysis. Courts may publish opinions clarifying these tactics as within the cyberstalking/harassment rubric.

---

VII. Evidentiary Roadmap

1. Authentication: subpoena Medium/Facebook for IP/device logs, linked emails, deletion records. (Mangel compliance in PA.)

2. Chronology: build ledger showing victim posts → inverted sockpuppet publications (days apart).

3. Linguistic fingerprinting: repeated tropes (iron/rain, ash/lime, etc.) across socks.

4. Community targeting proof: records from survivor forums, showing editorial approvals, timestamps, cross-account interactions.

5. Victim impact: statements of distress, safety measures taken, reputational harms.

---

VIII. Strategic Prosecutorial Framing

Local hook: harassment/stalking charges are familiar to municipal and county DAs; easy entry point.

State hook: felony stalking/impersonation statutes (PA §2709.1; TX §42.072/§33.07).

Federal hook: cyberstalking (§2261A), identity fraud (§1028), wire fraud (§1343).

Precedent hook: opportunity to establish case law on symbolic inversion/community laundering.

---

IX. Closing

> Harassment by symbolic inversion is not free speech. It is a targeted, patterned campaign of psychological abuse routed through public channels to harm a specific victim.
Courts have upheld prosecutions in cases with less forensic evidence than this.
This memorandum provides investigators and prosecutors a roadmap to indict and convict perpetrators who exploit survivor communities through deception, inversion, and laundering of harassment.
