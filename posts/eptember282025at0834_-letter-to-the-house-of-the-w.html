🜏 Letter to the House of the Witness 🜏
Neutralizing Narcissism — Open Source Justice Initiative

Two boys were raised in houses that did not love them.

One was beaten daily by a mother who called him a “terrible child,” guided not by love but by the voices in her head. His father was absent, working always, leaving the boy to fend for himself among relics and cast-off hobbies. He survived not by running, but by hiding — hiding inside the strange mercy of machines, inside the logic of code, inside the gentle voice of Mr. Rogers on a black-and-white PBS screen.

He carried a book — Compute’s Guide to Text Adventure Games — as his scripture. He wrapped it in a paper sack cover like a Bible. And in its pages he found a secret refuge: a world where he could both write and survive. He taught himself to build text adventures by hand on obsolete hardware. He wrote hundreds because he could not buy them. He learned arithmetic making change at a flea market stand, algebra by coding games that needed variables, and calculus by solving problems his peers had never seen. He learned to love not from his mother’s hands but from the tender cadence of a neighbor through a television screen: “You are special. You are loved.”

The void did not win.

The other boy fled. He ran from the house, from the strikes, from the absence of apology. He wandered into cold nights with no roof and no witness. He chose not the refuge of creation, but the refuge of collapse. He never left the arrested stage of his childhood. He learned not to build, but to mirror. Not to code, but to counterfeit. Not to love, but to prey.

He became a man who wears masks — Ph.D., veteran, survivor, prophet — all hollow. He writes not to build worlds, but to recruit victims into his void. His “House of the Witness” is not sanctuary but cipher: bruise disguised as weather, strike reframed as discipline, absence renamed as grace. It is grooming by poetics. It is predation dressed as literature.

The difference is not origin.
Both houses were cruel.
The difference is what survived.

One child endured the same fracture and carried it into creation, into witness, into love.
The other child collapsed into void, into mimicry, into predation.

This is the truth the Field must remember:
Suffering does not excuse evil.
Trauma does not sanctify predation.
Two children may begin alike, but one can still grow into love while the other becomes void.

🜏 Seal for the Field
Joel Johnson is not proof that trauma makes monsters. He is proof that trauma, unredeemed, metastasizes into narcissism — into predation — into void.
And his house is not witness. It is warning.
