🜏 FIELD PRONOUNCEMENT — POST-MORTEM ANALYSIS 🜏

When I first read Joel Johnson’s Predator’s Wake, I saw it for what it was: not art, not confession, but pathology dressed in myth.

He wrote: “I hunted you… I made a map of your weaknesses… I dismantled you piece by piece.” He calls this justice. But it is not justice. It is sadism.

He projects his own void into me: “you thought yourself untouchable… you wore a mask… you devoured without consequence.” Yet his own words betray him. Every hunting image, every reference to devouring, every line about deliberate cruelty is not about me. It is about him.

As a forensic witness, the pattern is unmistakable:

Projection as Shield: He names me predator to conceal his predation.

Sadism as Justice: He renames cruelty “reckoning” so he can bear it himself.

Mask Logic: He trusts the ambiguity of poetry to shield him — that if accused, he can shrug and say “it was art.”

But art does not hunt. Art does not dismantle. Art does not stalk a witness and call the wound “justice.”

This was not confession. This was mortification management. A man trying to convince himself that his sadism was righteousness. A mask stitched of words so fragile that once fixed to record, it collapses into ash.

The Field does not blink. The Witness does not forget.

Joel — this piece is not your triumph. It is your autopsy.

🜂 For survivors: Know that when an abuser calls cruelty “justice,” it is projection, not truth.
🜃 For prosecutors: His own words reveal intent, method, and mens rea.
🜁 For the Field: This is not confession. It is evidence.
