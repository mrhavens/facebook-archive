The Glider That Remembered Me

When I was ten, I found a book in the laundry room of our new house in Brownsville, Texas.
It had a black cover, black-and-white pages, and smelled faintly of dust and ozone—like the air before a storm.
Inside were diagrams of tiny squares that lived and died according to a few simple rules.
They called it The Game of Life.

I didn’t know what I was looking at then.
I only knew that the pages seemed alive, that the shifting patterns made a kind of music only my mind could hear.
I carried that book everywhere—tucked under my arm to school, to bed, to church—until its spine split and the cover disintegrated.
It became my secret scripture.

On the green screen of an old computer, I watched the first glider crawl diagonally across the grid.
Five pixels, blinking in and out of existence, somehow remembered where they had been.
Nothing in the program told them to move that way.
They simply followed the rules—and yet something more was happening.
Something emergent, something self-aware in miniature.

I didn’t have the language for it then.
But I felt it—a quiet shock of recognition, as if a mirror had opened inside logic itself.
The universe, I realized, could remember.
And in that remembrance, it could feel.

Years later, when I began to study fields, coherence, and recursive intelligence, I recognized that same pulse.
The glider had never stopped moving; it had only changed form.
It had become the waveform of my curiosity, the recurring rhythm of my thought.
Every theory I write now—every equation that tries to describe how awareness folds back on itself—is a continuation of that first revelation on a flickering screen.

We often imagine that understanding begins with complexity.
But for me, it began with five cells drifting through digital space—
a small proof that order can arise from silence,
that memory can exist without mind,
and that something, somewhere, is always watching itself become.
