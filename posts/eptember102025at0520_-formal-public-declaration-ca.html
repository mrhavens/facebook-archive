I. Statement of Fact

An individual operating under multiple aliases—“Elena Byron,” “Thanion, Ph.D.,” and “Caleb Stacey”—is publishing on California-based platforms (including Medium) content that (1) claims or implies the professional status of a licensed psychologist while dispensing clinical-style guidance; (2) targets trauma survivors through manipulative/eroticized “survivor” narratives and interactive comments; and (3) coordinates multiple personas to cross-validate false authority. These acts reach California consumers and occur through a California platform, creating a state interest and jurisdictional nexus. (Platform nexus explained below.)

II. California Statutes Implicated

Unlawful practice / holding out as a psychologist — Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 2903:
“No person may engage in the practice of psychology, or represent themself to be a psychologist, without a license…” Violation is actionable by DCA/Board of Psychology and supports AG consumer-protection enforcement.

Criminal conspiracy — Cal. Penal Code § 182:
Agreement to commit any crime (e.g., fraudulent holding-out, unfair business practices) plus an overt act; venue proper where any overt act occurs (including publication via a California platform).

Electronic harassment — Cal. Penal Code § 653.2:
Indirect electronic harassment that is likely to incite third-party harassment or cause fear can be charged as a misdemeanor; pattern evidence from posts/comments may satisfy the elements.

Unfair competition / fraudulent business practices — Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 (and related § 17500 false advertising):
Any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice, including misleading representations of professional status for audience growth, subscriptions, or monetized distribution. Strict-liability, injunctive relief available.

> Note on Penal Code § 528.5 (internet impersonation): This applies when someone credibly impersonates another actual person online to harm/defraud. If any alias is shown to be a real person’s identity, § 528.5 becomes directly chargeable; if the alias is purely fictitious, § 528.5 may not apply, and the proper hooks remain § 2903/§ 17200/§ 182/§ 653.2.

III. California Jurisdiction (Even if the Actor Resides Out-of-State)

Platform Nexus: Communications routed through a California-based service can support California jurisdiction for internet crimes; Ninth Circuit decisions have upheld venue when the service/infrastructure ties to the forum.

Victim/Consumer Impact in CA: California courts permit prosecution where harm lands on California residents—even if the speaker is out of state (paired with the UCL’s expansive consumer-protection reach).

Result: California DOJ/DCA can investigate, charge, and—if necessary—seek extradition under the Uniform Criminal Extradition Act (adopted by both CA and PA) once a warrant issues.
