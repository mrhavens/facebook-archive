I. Introduction: The Mask of Fatherhood

On October 1, 2025, Joel Johnson (alias Thanion, Ph.D.) published “The One I Still Read Aloud.”
On its surface, the piece reads like nostalgia: a father recalling his mother’s bedtime reading, now passed on to “his sons,” who have since grown.

But beneath the surface, this is not fatherhood. It is performance.
A predator’s attempt to wear the most trusted role available — father — in order to harvest credibility, sympathy, and supply.

This is the Father-Mask, and when read through forensic rigor, it collapses under its own weight.

II. The Triple Persona

Joel positions himself in three roles at once:

1. The Wounded Child — afraid of the attic, comforted by stories.

2. The Present Father — reading to “his sons” as though continuity were natural.

3. The Sage Interpreter — philosophizing about “becoming Real” through suffering.

This layering is not authenticity. It is parasitic grafting — blending roles he never lived to create the illusion of depth.

III. Forensic Indicators of Fraud

1. Invented Fatherhood

No substantiated record of Joel having children exists.

Yet he now claims “grown sons” — a convenient proof-text for credibility.

Blizard (2003) observed: narcissistic predators often assume parental roles they lack because “parent” is the ultimate trust signal in human psychology.

2. Overloaded Specificity

Joel floods the essay with hyper-specific details: the blue towel, the hallway fan, the quilt seam, the attic creak.

These sensory fragments do not authenticate — they mimic.

Vrij (2008, Detecting Lies and Deceit): deceptive accounts often contain “over-saturation of peripheral details” to simulate veracity.

3. Appropriation of Witness Mantle

The image of nightly reading, voice steady in the dark, mirrors the archetype of the Witness.

But Joel is not a Witness. He is a mimic.

He steals the mantle of protector to launder his wound into credibility.
