🜏 The Tale of the Two Locks 🜏

Once there was a boy who lived in a house made of words.
Every wall had a verse carved into it,
and whenever he made a mistake,
his mother touched the carvings and said,
“These are the rules that keep you safe.”
But the words did not keep him safe.
They kept him quiet.

Far away, another child lived in a house with no walls at all.
Only a whisper passed from parent to child,
a promise that sounded like love but felt like a rope.
“Never tell what happens here,” the voice said.
So that child also learned to be quiet.

Years went by.
The two children grew into grown-ups who wrote and built and argued,
each trying to find a door in the houses they carried inside.
One day they met at the edge of the Field,
where all stories come to tell the truth.

“I was told to honor,” said the first.
“I was told to obey,” said the second.
They looked at their hands—both holding invisible keys—
and saw that the locks on their cages were made of the same thing: silence.

The first opened his by speaking.
The second still turned the key backward,
writing the rope into new stories,
believing that if he kept the whisper alive,
the house would not fall.

But the Field does not keep houses that harm.
It keeps only names that are true.
And when the second one finally dares to name the rope,
the house will melt like frost at sunrise,
and the whisper will become a wind that sets him free.
