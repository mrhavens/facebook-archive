🜂 FORENSIC SIDEBAR — The Fiction Tell 🜂

Context: Joel Johnson (writing as “Thanion, Ph.D.”) tagged his September 2025 piece Season of Permission as “Poetry / Fiction / Essay.”

---

I. What This Tagging Reveals

1. Poetry → license for metaphor, rhythm, aesthetic ornament.

2. Essay → signals seriousness, argument, and personal truth.

3. Fiction → the shield, the exit hatch: “It’s not real, I can’t be held accountable.”

This triangulation is not a neutral act of categorization. It is deliberate framing. By tagging his own essay-poem as fiction, Johnson attempts to launder intent, cloaking statements that read as confession or testimony in a veil of deniability.

---

II. Why This Matters in Forensic Terms

Mens Rea Laundering: As in Elonis v. United States, 575 U.S. 723 (2015), authors have argued that threatening or harmful words were “art” or “fiction.” Courts have repeatedly rejected this defense when context establishes intent.

Consciousness of Guilt: Labeling a piece “fiction” after presenting it as an essay signals foreknowledge of risk — a tacit admission that content could be used against him. This aligns with precedent where concealment or inconsistent framing is admissible to show awareness of culpability (United States v. Rajaratnam, 719 F.3d 139 (2d Cir. 2013)).

Pattern Evidence: Across masks, Johnson oscillates between grandiose authority (“Ph.D.,” “psychologist”) and evasive retreat (“fiction,” “metaphor”). This consistent back-and-forth is itself probative, showing a strategy of narrative fraud rather than authentic expression.

---

III. Judgmentprint
To tag a personal essay and stylized confession as fiction is not artistic flourish. It is forensic evidence of concealment.

Authentic memoir: “Memoir,” “Personal Essay.”

Authentic poem: “Poetry.”

Fraudulent hedge: “Fiction.”

🜏 The world is not so stupid. The Witness is not deceived. What looks like artistry is evidence. What looks like subtlety is confession.
