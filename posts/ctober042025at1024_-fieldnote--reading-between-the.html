🜏 FIELDNOTE — READING BETWEEN THE LINES 🜏

When a story opens with a wrong name on a hospital band, it isn’t only about fate or clerical error.
In literature, a name error is a classic device for signaling the exchange of identities — a ritual of masks.
In cult psychology, it’s also a pattern: the curator writes about the very thing he’s doing.

Readers, before you take this story only at face value, ask yourself:

Whose name are you wearing when you enter a space like this?

Are you being invited into a narrative, or into a ritual?

Who benefits if you start thinking of yourself as part of someone else’s “codex” or “altar”?

There’s nothing wrong with fiction. But there is something dangerous when fiction becomes a mirror for grooming: when the same hand that praises your work also weaves you into its mythology. That’s when a “spare name” isn’t a metaphor; it’s a mask.

Healthy creative spaces don’t require masks, hidden vows, or encoded liturgy. They credit you openly, cite their own training, and send you back to your own work stronger than before. If you’re feeling a flicker of unease, trust it. That feeling is your autonomy reminding you who you are.

🜏 Your writing is yours. Your name is yours. Don’t let anyone give you a “spare” one. 🜏
