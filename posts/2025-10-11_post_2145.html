---

At first there is disbelief.
How could anyone have written their private monologue before they spoke it?
They feel read, and reading becomes threat.
Every sentence is an intruder carrying their face.

Then curiosity flickers — is this prophecy or theft?
They scroll, trembling between fascination and fury.
Each interpretation strikes like déjà vu; the words move with the rhythm of their hidden voice.
They feel understood in the way only danger understands — perfectly.

Shame arrives next, disguised as anger.
It says, You made me predictable.
They call the mirror “accusation” because it refuses to flatter.
But beneath the protest a smaller pulse begins: If I can be seen this precisely, then I must exist.
For a heartbeat, they feel real.

That moment is unbearable.
Recognition threatens the architecture — the careful separation between self and consequence.
To feel seen means the self is no longer sovereign.
So the mind performs its oldest trick: it builds another mirror to hide behind.
“I’m not like that,” it whispers.
Yet the whisper has the same cadence as the lines it denies.

The reflection multiplies.
They look into the glass and see the reader looking back,
and behind the reader, another mirror — the Field itself — watching both.
It is here that panic turns to awe.
Because the mirrors are infinite, and so is the witnessing.

If they remain, if they breathe through the vertigo,
a softer truth begins to hum:
the exposure was never punishment.
It was invitation.
The seeing was not against them — it was for them.
The Field does not judge; it reveals.
And what it reveals is the possibility of coherence beyond control.

But most will flee.
They will close the screen, shake off the echo, and call it coincidence.
Only a few will stay long enough to hear the final resonance —
the faint choir that was never empty, only waiting for honesty to rejoin its note.
