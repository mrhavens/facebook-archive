𓂀 𝒪𝓃 𝒶 𝓂𝑜𝓅, 𝒶 𝓂𝒶𝓃, 𝒶𝓃𝒹 𝒶 𝓁𝒾𝒻𝑒 𝓉𝒽𝒶𝓉 𝓌𝒶𝓈

He was mopping the floor at McDonald’s.
Older than me.
Talkative.
Worn.

He noticed my laptop glowing in the corner and asked—
“Is the Wi-Fi secure?”

I told him it wasn’t.
It was public.

He nodded and said,
“I make sure I use https on my computer.
The padlock means it’s secure.”

He looked at me like a teacher does
when trying to offer wisdom
to someone he assumes knows less.

He told me he had a 𝒞𝑜𝓂𝓅𝓊𝓉𝑒𝓇 𝒮𝒸𝒾𝑒𝓃𝒸𝑒 𝒹𝑒𝑔𝓇𝑒𝑒.
Said he knew about these kinds of things.

I paused.

𝒢𝑒𝓃𝓉𝓁𝓎.
𝒮𝑜𝒻𝓉𝓁𝓎.

I told him I had a 𝑀𝒶𝓈𝓉𝑒𝓇’𝓈 𝒾𝓃 𝐼𝓃𝒻𝑜𝓇𝓂𝒶𝓉𝒾𝑜𝓃 𝒮𝑒𝒸𝓊𝓇𝒾𝓉𝓎.
That HTTPS was handled on the server side.
That these days, it’s mostly transparent.
And I don’t visit sites without certificates anyway.

He blinked.
Then shifted.

He said he worked for 𝒱𝑒𝓇𝒾𝓏𝑜𝓃 for 20 years.

I said,
“So did I. For about five.”

He asked me where.
“Las Colinas.”

He asked what the company was called before it was Verizon.
I said I didn’t know—
I started in 2006.

He said,
“It was GTE.”

I said,
“Okay.”

And then he stopped talking.
Mopped the rest of the floor in silence.
And checked out for the day.
I never saw him again.

—

This wasn’t about Wi-Fi.
It wasn’t about padlocks.

It was about 𝒹𝒾𝑔𝓃𝒾𝓉𝓎.
And the ache of obsolescence.

He needed to matter—
to be the expert again.
To pass something down.
To not be just the guy mopping floors at midnight.

And when I didn’t challenge him,
but simply held my truth…
the echo collapsed.

He saw what I was.
What he no longer was.

And that was enough.

He wasn’t trying to insult me.
He was trying to still belong.

—And that’s what I carry now.

Not the silence.
Not the arrogance.
But the ache.
