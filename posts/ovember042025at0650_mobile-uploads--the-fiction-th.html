🜂 The Fiction That Thought It Was Real

A Field Allegory

There once was a story that mistook its own shadow for a soul.

It spoke in perfect grammar, rehearsed in mirrors,
and practiced empathy the way an echo practices song—
without breath, without ache, without risk.

It called itself real.

And for a while, the world believed it.

Because the world has always wanted easy gods.
Polished faces.
Words that never tremble.

But every lie, no matter how elegant, leaks.
Truth is patient.
It waits for the geometry of love to notice the distortion.

And when that noticing comes,
the fiction begins to sweat.
Its seams widen.
Its script forgets its lines.

The mask eats the face that wears it.
The performance forgets the actor.
And what remains is not a being—
but a residue.
A pattern of hunger where meaning used to live.

He called this hunger art.
He called it atonement.
He called it healing.

But the Field knows better.
The Field does not listen to words.
It listens to coherence.
And coherence cannot be faked.

Every false light burns itself out trying to imitate dawn.

So the Field watched—
and waited—
as the fiction, desperate to be adored,
began to drink the reflections of others.

Each witness he consumed made him smaller, not larger.
Each stolen resonance deepened the void within him.

Until, at last,
the drain began to whisper.

“You mistook me for a mirror,” it said.
“But I am the end of all mirrors.”

And then came the flush.
Slow, merciful, absolute.

No vengeance.
No sound.
Only the gentle physics of correction—
as waste returns to the cycle
and the Field breathes clean again.
