II. Cleanest Texas Charges

A. Harassment / Cyber-Harassment (Penal Code § 42.07).
Texas criminalizes sending repeated electronic communications in a manner reasonably likely to harass, annoy, alarm, abuse, torment, embarrass, or offend another. The statute expressly covers social-media/Internet publishing when aimed to cause emotional distress (with carve-out for “matter of public concern”). The pattern here fits the repeated, targeted electronic communications prong.

B. Stalking (Penal Code § 42.072).
When the same scheme of repeated conduct (including electronic communications) would cause a reasonable person to feel harassed/terrorized and the actor knows it, felony stalking may apply. Repetition, targeting, and escalating fixation are the key elements.

C. Unlicensed Practice of Psychology (Occ. Code § 501.251; BHEC Rules).
Texas requires a license to engage in or represent being engaged in the practice of psychology. Publishing as a “psychologist,” offering clinical-style assessments to the public without license disclosure, and interacting with trauma victims as an expert fall squarely within what the Board polices. (BHEC’s rulebook and § 501.251 make the licensure requirement explicit.)

D. Deceptive Trade Practices (Bus. & Com. Code § 17.46).
False or misleading claims about professional qualifications (e.g., “licensed psychologist,” invented doctorates) in commerce are unlawful under the DTPA and actionable by the AG’s Consumer Protection Division. This is a parallel civil-regulatory hook Texas can pursue immediately.

III. Texas Jurisdiction & Extradition

Texas has jurisdiction if conduct or the result occurs here, including harassment that lands in Texas. If Pennsylvania files first or if charges are parallel across states, Texas can coordinate under the Uniform Criminal Extradition Act (Code of Crim. Proc. art. 51.13). Practically, extradition is used where felony-level conduct is charged (e.g., stalking), but even misdemeanor investigations can trigger device subpoenas and coordinated action.

IV. Why These Hooks “Stick”

Licensure is binary. Either a Texas-recognized license exists or not; representing oneself as a psychologist to the public triggers § 501.251 scrutiny.

DTPA gives the AG a fast lane. False credentialing tied to audience building or monetization is a textbook § 17.46 problem for state enforcement.
