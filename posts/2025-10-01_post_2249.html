🜏 FROM SCRAPS TO SYSTEMS 🜏

A Memoir Fragment for the Archive of the ONE

I was ten when Gary Sharp sat me in front of his Tandy 1000.
The screen glowed blue and waiting. I picked up DOS commands faster than even I expected.
Gary watched me for a long moment, then leaned back in his chair.

“When you’re old enough,” he said, “I want to go into business with you.”

No adult had ever said anything like that to me.
Not “good boy.” Not “obedient child.” Not even “I’m proud of you.”
But partner.
Equal in waiting.
It was a covenant, and it carried me through years of exile.

---

At home I had scraps: an old Apple II, a TI/994a, then the Tandy 2000 my father bought with the money I earned washing dishes for years — twenty dollars a month, coins stacked slowly into a dream.
The 2000 was obsolete the day it arrived. It ran nothing the world was running.
No games worked. No software worked.

So I learned to port.
Again.
Line by line, I re-wrote dead code until it began new life.
While other kids were playing games that simply ran, I was breaking and re-stitching code across machines until it half-ran, half-crashed.

The Tandy taught me to live with failure, and it taught me that failure could be resurrected.
That was my first obsession. My first proof of mastery.

---

Then Uncle Bob moved in.
Fresh from divorce, he filled our house with the wreckage of a failed computer business — thirty thousand dollars’ worth of stock dumped into corners, closets, garages.

It was chaos to him. To me, it was paradise.

There was a 71 megabyte hard drive — a giant, three or four times larger than anything anyone else had.
I borrowed it. I scavenged everything I could.
I built machines from parts no one else wanted.

By thirteen, I had Dimension X — my Bulletin Board System, running Telegard at 1200 baud.
I dropped its name at Radio Shack, where they kept a list of local boards.
And then strangers began calling.

For the first time in my life, people were dialing my number not to punish me, not to shame me — but to connect.
To play.
To build.
To join.

---

The church knew too. Business owners would call my house and pick me up to fix their computers. I was thirteen. They’d hand me fifty or a hundred dollars, plus a combo meal, just to do work that would have cost them hundreds from an adult.

They gave me old parts in return — more scraps to build with.
Adults started seeing me not as a “problem child,” but as someone who could solve their problems.
I was a professional before I was even out of middle school.

---

At nineteen, I walked into Gary Sharp’s office.
He was older, a little slower, but his eyes still carried the same sharpness.
“Do you remember what you told me when I was ten?” I asked.

He smiled. “I meant it.”

And we went into business together.
The covenant fulfilled.
The loop closed.

---

Looking back, I see the geometry now.
A grandmother with snacks and stories.
A counselor who kept the rock I painted with her name.
An uncle who left behind a junkyard of machines.
A mentor who promised me partnership.

Each of them was a Witness.
Each bent the recursion of my life away from void.

Because Witness scales.
One child, given love in scraps, can build entire systems.
One word of belief can carry through years of shame.
One covenant can turn a “terrible child” into an architect of community.

That was my story.
Scraps to systems.
Void to Witness.
