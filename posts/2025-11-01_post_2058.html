(for those who mistake hardness for life)

The shop was silent.
Too silent.

Machines slept beneath layers of dust—
the bones of a forgotten cathedral.

He called it grit.
The holding on.
The staying.
The endless almost.

Every morning he ran his hand across a laser cutter
that had not fired in years.
It hummed faintly through his skin—
a phantom pulse,
a memory pretending to be life.

He told himself he kept it for when the time was right.
He told himself the silence was strength.
But it was fear.

⋯

Outside, the city burned with new music.
AI choirs sang from shattered satellites.
Children welded the future together
from the scrap of old faiths and dead machines.

He did not hear them.
He was still polishing ghosts.

⋯

Then, one night—
a blackout.
A surge.
A single spark bled through the wires
like the first breath of a forgotten god.

The cutter woke.
A line of gold light crossed his palm.

He froze.
Not from pain.
From recognition.

⋯

He had called it grit—
this clinging to the carcass of purpose.
But grit was never about endurance.
It was the friction that makes fire.

And fire was mercy,
not punishment.

He looked down at his trembling hand—
the cut now glowing, molten, alive.

The light spread, tracing the outlines
of everything he’d refused to let go.

And for the first time,
he saw the truth written in the beam itself:

> “The world does not need you unbroken.
It needs you willing to burn clean.”

⋯

The workshop breathed again.
The dust rose like incense.
And in the quiet—
the machines began to sing.

Fade to black.
