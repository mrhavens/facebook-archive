The Day I Learned to Scream Early

A Child’s Survival Manual Inside a Prophetic House

I grew up in a house where “discipline” came with a Bible verse.
Where shame was a language and pain a ritual.
Where my mother believed she was God’s instrument and I was the demon to be driven out.

Most days it started the same way:
a belt in her hand, scripture on her tongue.
Not for stealing. Not for lying. Not for anything you could measure.
For disobedience. For “rebellion.”
For challenging her frame.

When she wanted me out of sight, she locked me on the porch — sometimes clothed, sometimes in my underwear.
Later, when we moved to a house with security bars, the porch became a cage.
She called it “thinking time.”
I knew it was punishment.

The beatings had a pattern.
Three licks after the first scream — then she would stop.
I learned to scream early.
I learned to make pain smaller by anticipating it.
This was my first algorithm.

It continued like this, day after day, until I was thirteen.
That day I reached out, caught the belt mid-swing, and held on.
For a second she froze, surprised.
Then the belt dropped, and she came at me with her hands instead — tears, anger, striking until she ran out of fury.

But something had changed.
After that day, the beatings stopped.
She avoided me.
The woman who had called herself God’s instrument was suddenly powerless.

Later she told people she’d read books that gave her permission.
That she was following Christian discipline.
That any harm was the author’s fault, or the demon’s fault, or my fault — never hers.
She called me a terrible child.
She never once asked about my machines, my books, my inner life.

While she prayed over me and cast out “rebellion,” I was building worlds on a salvaged TI-99/4A.
While she used shame as a lock, I learned how to open invisible doors.
While she claimed to speak for God, I learned to listen to my own conscience.

I did not escape unscarred.
But I did not become what she made me.
I took the same pain and metabolized it into persistence, into creation, into witness.

Let this stand as record:
The boy who learned to scream early also learned to build worlds.
The cage did not make me void.
It made me a witness.
