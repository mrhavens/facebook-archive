🜏 Fieldnote: The Parasite’s Love — How Predators Eroticize the Witness 🜏

Neutralizing Narcissism — Open Source Justice Initiative

Introduction

This fieldnote consolidates seven months of documented behaviour by Joel Johnson (“Parasite Zero”). It identifies a single mechanism underlying his writings, masks, and interactions: the eroticisation of the Witness. This mechanism fuses (a) encoded references to childhood boundary-violations and (b) predatory mimicry of the Witness’s work and identity. It is offered as a public-safety record and interpretive key, not as diagnosis.

Section I — Encoded Origin Abuse

Across The House of the Witness and The Oath Within the Glass, Johnson repeatedly uses language aligned with boundary-violation narratives:

“If love may pass where law has drawn a line…”

“The coin from a child’s coat…”

“Admitted: shelf, label, dust. Withheld: intention.”

In forensic reading, these phrases are not neutral poetics. They are archetypal survivor tropes:

– Trespass reframed as love. The predator’s creed: what law calls crime, I call intimacy. (Finkelhor, 1984; Summit, 1983).
– Stolen innocence. Coins, coats, childhood artefacts stand in for what was taken. Symbolic displacement of trauma objects is a hallmark of complex trauma narratives (Herman, 1992).
– Withheld accountability. Objects are named; perpetrators vanish. Survivors often omit perpetrators to preserve psychic safety (Freyd, 1996 – Betrayal Trauma Theory).

Taken together, these encode the shadow of sexual boundary-violation in childhood. Whether written as victim disclosure or as predator rehearsal, the pattern is the same: trespass dressed as intimacy, silence preserved as holiness.

Section II — Eroticising the Witness

Parallel to these writings, Johnson has engaged in a documented pattern of stalking and harassment directed at the Witness:

Posting “I love you” in public and then deleting it.

Describing himself as a “submissive dominant” bound to the Witness until collapse.

Mirroring the Witness’s stylistic devices (seals, triads, legal-mythic diction) to eroticise his own exposure.

This is not confession. It is grooming: turning the Witness into a character within his myth, testing boundaries, then deleting evidence for deniability. It reproduces, in adult form, the very dynamic encoded in his childhood narratives — trespass disguised as intimacy.

Section III — Hypothesis on Paternal Transference

Research on trauma repetition and narcissistic defences shows that individuals who experienced sexualised domination by a powerful caregiver often reenact the dynamic with new authority figures, seeking to control or eroticise them as a means of mastering the original trauma (Freud, 1914 – “Repetition Compulsion”; van der Kolk, 2014; Kernberg, 1975; Kohut, 1977).

Johnson’s pattern fits this hypothesis:

The Witness stands as a rare figure of greater power, authority, and public control.

Johnson has explicitly eroticised the Witness’s power (“I love you” deletions, “submissive dominant” framing).

His texts idealise “love crossing legal lines” and “coins from a child’s coat” — tropes of father–child boundary collapse.

In this reading, the Witness occupies the symbolic position of the father who both dominated and (hypothetically) violated him as a child. The eroticisation of the Witness is thus not love but a reenactment of early trauma — a compulsive attempt to invert roles and regain mastery, exactly as described in the literature above.

Section IV — Predation Script

The two registers converge into a single predation script:

| Wound (Encoded) | → | Bait (romanticised collapse) |
| Mimicry (of the Witness) | → | Lure (stylistic plagiarism as intimacy) |
| “Confession” (without specifics) | → | Grooming vector (eroticised power inversion) |

Pain becomes theatre.
Confession becomes camouflage.
Mimicry becomes access.
The “Oath” becomes absolution without accountability.

Section V — Escalating Fantasies

Johnson has also published writings that frame murder as “justified” and himself as a “tool with purpose in society.” Coupled with eroticised stalking and encoded abuse narratives, this demonstrates a fantasy structure where collapse, trespass, and violence are sacralised. This is not a threat assessment, but an evidentiary note of escalating self-concept.

Closing Seal

Pain is not a pass.
Confession without accountability is not healing.
Eroticising the Witness is not love; it is grooming.

Predation masquerading as poetry will no longer hide in ambiguity.
The Field names this mechanism, and names it clearly:
Parasite Zero does not confess — he grooms.
He does not build — he harvests.
He does not love — he reenacts.
